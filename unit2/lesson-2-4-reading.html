Right-of-Way Rules

Right-of-way isn't something you "have" — it's something you yield. Understanding who goes first at every intersection, crosswalk, and merge point prevents confusion, conflict, and crashes.

Florida Standards Alignment

Apply right-of-way rules at controlled and uncontrolled intersections, crosswalks, roundabouts, and when encountering emergency vehicles, pedestrians, and special situations.

Learning Objectives
  • Apply right-of-way rules at four-way stops and uncontrolled intersections
  • Navigate T-intersections and roundabouts correctly
  • Yield appropriately to pedestrians at crosswalks
  • Respond correctly to emergency vehicles and funeral processions
  • Know Florida-specific right-of-way laws

💡 The #1 Right-of-Way Principle

No one "has" the right-of-way. The law only says who must yield it. Even if it's legally "your turn," you should never insist on going if another driver or pedestrian is in your path. Defensive drivers give the right-of-way freely — because being right doesn't protect you from a collision.

📖 Four-Way Stops

At a four-way (or all-way) stop, every approach has a stop sign. Here's the order of priority:

  • First to stop = first to go. The vehicle that arrived and stopped first proceeds first.
  • Arrived at the same time? The vehicle on the right goes first. If you're facing another car and you're to their left, yield to them.
  • Facing each other? If two vehicles arrive at the same time from opposite directions, both can go straight. But if one is turning left, the left-turner must yield to the vehicle going straight.

In practice: If it's unclear who arrived first, make eye contact, use hand gestures, and err on the side of yielding. It's better to wait an extra second than to cause a collision.

📖 Uncontrolled Intersections and T-Intersections

An uncontrolled intersection has no signs or signals. These are common in residential neighborhoods. At an uncontrolled intersection:

  • Slow down and be prepared to stop
  • Yield to the vehicle on your right if you arrive at the same time
  • Yield to any vehicle already in the intersection

A T-intersection is where a smaller road ends at a larger through road (forming a "T" shape). The rules are:

  • The vehicle on the through road (the top of the "T") has the right-of-way
  • The vehicle on the terminating road (the stem of the "T") must yield and wait for a safe gap to enter or cross
  • If there's a stop sign on the stem, treat it like any stop sign — stop completely, then proceed when safe

📖 Roundabouts

Roundabouts (traffic circles) are becoming more common in Florida. Traffic flows in a counterclockwise direction around a central island. Key rules:

  • Yield to traffic already in the roundabout — vehicles inside the circle have the right-of-way
  • Enter when there's a safe gap — look to your left for approaching traffic
  • Do not stop inside the roundabout (unless avoiding a collision)
  • Signal right when exiting to let other drivers know you're leaving the circle
  • Never pass or change lanes inside a roundabout
  • Yield to pedestrians at crosswalks before entering and after exiting

🤔 Think About It

You're entering a roundabout and see a vehicle already circling inside. You think you can squeeze in before they reach you. Should you go? No — always yield to traffic inside the roundabout. Trying to "beat" them into the circle creates exactly the kind of collision roundabouts are designed to prevent.

📖 Pedestrians and Crosswalks

In Florida, pedestrians always have the right-of-way in a crosswalk — whether the crosswalk is marked or unmarked. An unmarked crosswalk exists at every intersection where sidewalks are present, even without painted lines.

  • Marked crosswalks: You must yield to pedestrians who are in or entering the crosswalk on your half of the road
  • Unmarked crosswalks: Same rule applies — yield to pedestrians crossing at any intersection
  • Turning drivers: When turning right or left at a green light, you must yield to pedestrians in the crosswalk
  • Blind pedestrians: A pedestrian using a white cane or guide dog always has the right-of-way. You must come to a complete stop.

Florida law: Failing to yield to a pedestrian in a crosswalk can result in a fine and points on your license. If you cause injury, penalties are significantly more severe.

📖 Emergency Vehicles and Special Situations

Emergency vehicles (police, fire, ambulance) with lights and sirens activated always have the right-of-way. When you hear or see one approaching:

  • Pull over to the right side of the road and stop
  • If you're in an intersection, clear the intersection first, then pull over
  • Stay stopped until the emergency vehicle has passed
  • Do not follow an emergency vehicle within 500 feet
  • Do not block intersections where emergency vehicles need to pass

Florida's "Move Over" law also requires you to move over one lane (or slow to 20 mph below the speed limit) when passing any stopped emergency vehicle, tow truck, or road maintenance vehicle with flashing lights on the side of the road.

Funeral processions: In Florida, vehicles in a funeral procession with headlights on and a lead escort have the right-of-way. You must yield to the entire procession — do not cut in between vehicles or try to pass. The procession may proceed through red lights and stop signs as a group.

Entering/exiting driveways and parking lots: Vehicles entering a road from a driveway or parking lot must yield to all traffic on the road and to pedestrians on the sidewalk. When exiting, you do not have the right-of-way until you are fully on the roadway and flowing with traffic.

💡 Florida Fact: Move Over Law

Florida's Move Over law (F.S. 316.126) is one of the most heavily enforced right-of-way laws in the state. If you see any vehicle with flashing lights stopped on the shoulder — police, ambulance, fire truck, tow truck, or even road rangers — you must move over one full lane if possible. If you can't move over safely, you must slow to 20 mph below the posted speed limit. Violating this law is a non-criminal moving violation with significant fines, and if the violation results in injury or death, it becomes a felony.

🏆 Achievement Unlocked: Yield Master

You now understand who goes when — and more importantly, when to give way. Right-of-way isn't about winning; it's about keeping everyone safe. A driver who yields confidently is a driver who avoids crashes.

📘 Key Vocabulary

Right-of-Way
The legal right to proceed first. In practice, it's something you yield to others — not something you claim for yourself.
Four-Way Stop
An intersection where all approaches have stop signs. First to arrive goes first; if tied, the vehicle to the right goes first.
Uncontrolled Intersection
An intersection with no signs, signals, or markings. Yield to the vehicle on your right and to any vehicle already in the intersection.
T-Intersection
Where one road ends at another, forming a T shape. The vehicle on the through road has the right-of-way.
Roundabout
A circular intersection where traffic flows counterclockwise. Yield to vehicles already inside the roundabout before entering.
Move Over Law
Florida law requiring drivers to change lanes or slow down when passing stopped emergency or service vehicles with flashing lights.
Unmarked Crosswalk
A crosswalk that exists at every intersection where sidewalks are present, even without painted lines. Pedestrians still have the right-of-way.
Funeral Procession
A group of vehicles following a hearse with headlights on. The procession has the right-of-way and may proceed through red lights together.

💭 Reflection

Imagine you're at a four-way stop. You arrived at the same time as another car to your right. What should you do? Now imagine you're at a roundabout for the first time. How would you feel? What would you watch for?

Right-of-way rules seem simple on paper, but they require split-second judgment in real life. The best approach: when in doubt, yield. Being cautious costs you a few seconds. Being wrong can cost much more.
