Traffic Signals and Pavement Markings

Traffic lights tell you when to go. Pavement markings tell you where to go. Together, they keep millions of drivers, cyclists, and pedestrians moving safely through the most complex parts of every road — intersections.

Florida Standards Alignment

Explain the meaning of traffic signals (including arrows and flashing signals) and pavement markings, and describe the correct driver response to each.

Learning Objectives
  • Identify the meaning of steady and flashing traffic signals
  • Explain arrow signal rules for turning movements
  • Interpret pedestrian signals and crossing rules
  • Distinguish between solid and dashed lane markings
  • Recognize special pavement markings including turn lanes, bike lanes, and HOV lanes

📖 Traffic Lights: Red, Yellow, Green

Traffic lights (also called signals) are the primary way intersections are controlled. Every new driver must know exactly what each light means — not just the color, but whether it's steady or flashing.

Steady (solid) lights:

  • Steady green — You may proceed through the intersection if it's clear. You may also turn left or right unless a sign prohibits it. Left-turners must yield to oncoming traffic.
  • Steady yellow — The light is about to turn red. If you can stop safely before the intersection, you must stop. If you're already in the intersection or too close to stop safely, proceed with caution.
  • Steady red — Stop completely behind the stop line or crosswalk. Remain stopped until the light turns green. Exception: You may turn right on red after a complete stop, unless a sign says "No Turn on Red."

Flashing lights:

  • Flashing red — Treat it exactly like a stop sign. Stop completely, check for traffic, then proceed when safe.
  • Flashing yellow — Proceed with caution. Slow down and watch for cross traffic and pedestrians, but you do not need to stop.

🤔 Think About It

You're approaching an intersection at 40 mph and the light turns yellow. You're about 3 car lengths from the intersection. Should you speed up to "make the light" or brake to stop? The answer: if you can stop safely, you must stop. Running a yellow that turns red is a violation — and a common cause of serious intersection crashes.

📖 Arrow Signals

Arrow signals control specific turning movements at an intersection. They appear alongside or instead of regular circular lights.

  • Green arrow — You have a protected turn. Oncoming traffic has a red light, so you may turn in the direction of the arrow without waiting for a gap. Still check for pedestrians.
  • Yellow arrow — Your protected turn is ending. Prepare to stop. If you're already in the intersection, complete your turn safely.
  • Red arrow — Do not turn in the direction of the arrow. Wait until the arrow turns green or you get a green circular signal. In Florida, you cannot turn right on a red arrow.
  • Flashing yellow arrow — You may turn in the direction of the arrow, but you must yield to oncoming traffic and pedestrians. This is an unprotected turn — no one is being stopped for you.

💡 Florida Fact

In Florida, you may turn right on a steady red light after a complete stop, unless a sign prohibits it. However, you cannot turn on a red arrow — a red arrow means absolutely no turning in that direction until the signal changes. Many Florida intersections now use flashing yellow left-turn arrows, which allow you to turn left when there's a gap in oncoming traffic.

📖 Pedestrian Signals

Pedestrian signals are mounted at crosswalks and display symbols or text telling people when to walk. As a driver, understanding these signals helps you predict when pedestrians might be crossing:

  • Walking person (white) — Pedestrians may begin crossing. Drivers turning through the crosswalk must yield.
  • Flashing hand with countdown — Pedestrians already in the crosswalk should finish crossing; new pedestrians should not start. Drivers must still yield to anyone in the crosswalk.
  • Steady hand (orange/red) — Do not cross. But drivers must still yield to any pedestrian who is still in the crosswalk.

Key rule for drivers: Regardless of what the pedestrian signal shows, you must always yield to pedestrians in a crosswalk. In Florida, failing to yield to a pedestrian in a crosswalk is a traffic violation with significant fines.

📖 Pavement Markings: Lines on the Road

Pavement markings are painted directly on the road surface. They're just as important as signs and signals — they tell you where you can drive, where you can pass, and where you need to be. There are two main colors:

  • Yellow lines — Separate traffic moving in opposite directions (oncoming traffic). They're always in the center of a two-way road.
  • White lines — Separate traffic moving in the same direction or mark the edge of the road.

Yellow line rules:

  • Broken yellow center line — Passing is allowed (when safe and legal)
  • Solid yellow center line — No passing on your side. If the solid line is on your side and a broken line is on the other side, you may not pass — but the other direction can.
  • Double solid yellow lines — No passing in either direction. You may cross them only to turn into a driveway or side road.

White line rules:

  • Broken white lines — Lane changes are permitted
  • Solid white lines — Discourage (or prohibit) lane changes. Stay in your lane.
  • Double solid white lines — Lane changes are prohibited

📖 Special Pavement Markings

Beyond basic lane lines, you'll encounter several special markings:

  • Crosswalks — White parallel lines or "zebra stripe" patterns at intersections. Pedestrians have the right of way here.
  • Stop lines — Thick white lines before crosswalks. Stop behind this line — not on it or past it.
  • Turn lanes — Arrows painted on the pavement showing which direction that lane goes (left turn, right turn, or straight only).
  • Center left-turn lane — A lane bordered by solid yellow on the outside and broken yellow on the inside. Shared by traffic from both directions for left turns only. Never use it as a driving lane.
  • Bike lanes — Marked with a bicycle symbol and solid white line. Do not drive in bike lanes except when making a right turn (and only in the last 200 feet before the turn).
  • HOV lanes (High Occupancy Vehicle) — Marked with a diamond symbol (◇) on the pavement and signs. Restricted to vehicles with 2 or more occupants during designated hours.
  • Rumble strips — Textured or raised strips along the shoulder or center line that vibrate your vehicle to alert you if you drift out of your lane.

💡 Florida Fact

Florida's I-95 and I-595 in South Florida feature express lanes separated by double solid white lines and pylons. These lanes have restricted entry and exit points — you cannot cross the double white lines to enter or exit. Look for designated opening points marked with dashed white lines and signs. Entering or exiting outside these points is a traffic violation.

🏆 Achievement Unlocked: Signal Master

You now understand traffic signals and pavement markings — the two systems that keep intersections running. Every time you approach an intersection, you'll read the light, check the arrows, scan the crosswalk markings, and position yourself in the correct lane. That's driving smart.

📘 Key Vocabulary

Protected Turn
A turn made on a green arrow signal, where oncoming traffic is stopped by a red light.
Unprotected Turn
A turn where you must yield to oncoming traffic — typically on a solid green or flashing yellow arrow.
Flashing Yellow Arrow
A signal that permits turning, but requires you to yield to oncoming traffic and pedestrians.
Double Solid Yellow Lines
Center line markings that prohibit passing in both directions. May be crossed only for turning into a driveway.
Center Left-Turn Lane
A shared lane in the middle of the road used only for making left turns, bordered by solid and dashed yellow lines.
HOV Lane
High Occupancy Vehicle lane — restricted to vehicles with 2+ occupants, marked with a diamond symbol.
Crosswalk
A marked area of roadway where pedestrians may cross. Drivers must yield to pedestrians in a crosswalk.
Rumble Strips
Textured patterns on the road shoulder or center line that vibrate your car to alert you of lane departure.

💭 Reflection

Think about a busy intersection you know well. What types of signals does it have? Are there arrow signals for turning? What about pavement markings — can you picture the lane lines, crosswalks, and turn arrows?

Next time you're riding through that intersection, try to notice every signal and marking. Count the number of different signals you can see from one approach. You might be surprised at how much information the road is giving you — and how much drivers rely on it without even thinking about it.
